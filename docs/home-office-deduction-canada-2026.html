Home Office Deduction Canada 2026

How employees and self-employed Canadians can claim home office expenses and reduce their tax bill

Smart Canada Tax · May 2026 · 6 min read
← Back to Blog

If you work from home — whether as an employee or self-employed — you may be able to deduct a portion of your home expenses from your taxable income. The rules are different depending on whether you are an employee or run your own business. This guide covers both.

Note: The COVID-era flat-rate home office method ($2/day) ended after the 2022 tax year. For 2023 onwards, only the detailed method applies. You must have a T2200 signed by your employer if you are an employee.

Employees — Home Office Deduction Rules 2026

Who qualifies?

As an employee, you can claim home office expenses only if:

  • You were required to work from home under your employment contract, or your employer required it
  • You use part of your home exclusively and regularly for work
  • Your employer completed and signed Form T2200 (Declaration of Conditions of Employment)

What expenses can employees claim?

Rent (portion of home)YesYes
Heat, electricity, waterYesYes

Maintenance and minor repairsYesYes

How to calculate the work-space percentage

Use whichever method applies:

  • Area method: Home office area ÷ total home area × 100%
  • Rooms method: Number of rooms used for work ÷ total rooms × 100%

Example: Your home is 1,200 sq ft. Your dedicated office is 120 sq ft. You can claim 10% of eligible home expenses. If your monthly hydro + heat + internet = $400, you can claim $40/month × 12 = $480/year.

Self-Employed — Home Office Deduction Rules 2026

Self-employed Canadians (sole proprietors, freelancers, contractors) have more generous home office rules than employees — and no T2200 is required.

Who qualifies?

Your home workspace must be either:

  • Your principal place of business — where you do most of your work, OR
  • Used exclusively and on a regular and continuous basis for meeting clients, customers, or patients

What expenses can self-employed claim?

  • Rent or mortgage interest (not principal)
  • Property taxes
  • Heat, electricity, water
  • Home insurance
  • Internet (business portion)
  • Maintenance and repairs
  • CCA (Capital Cost Allowance) on the home — though this can affect the principal residence exemption

Warning for homeowners: Claiming CCA on your home office can reduce your principal residence exemption when you sell. Consult a tax professional before claiming CCA on your home. For renters, this concern does not apply.

Calculation example (self-employed)

Home: 1,500 sq ft. Office: 150 sq ft. Work-space percentage: 10%.

  • Monthly rent: $2,500 × 10% = $250/month = $3,000/year
  • Annual utilities: $3,600 × 10% = $360/year
  • Annual internet ($100/month): business portion 80% = $960/year
  • Total deduction: ~$4,320/year

Key Rules to Remember

  • Home office expenses can only reduce business income — they cannot create a loss (employees). Unused amounts carry forward to next year.
  • The space must be used exclusively for work — a kitchen table where you also eat dinner does not qualify.
  • Keep all receipts and records for at least 6 years in case of a CRA audit.
  • Internet expense should be split between personal and business use — CRA will question 100% business claims.

Form T2200 — What Employers Need to Sign

Employees must have their employer complete Form T2200 before claiming home office expenses. The form confirms:

  • The employee was required to work from home
  • The employee pays their own home office expenses
  • The employer does not reimburse these expenses

You do not submit T2200 with your tax return — keep it in case CRA asks for it. Employers are required to provide T2200 if conditions are met.
